\(\blert{\text{Method 1}}\)
We use the fact that corresponding sides of similar figures are proportional.
\begin{gather*} \hphantom{large parallelogram0000} \text{short sides} ~~\text{long sides}\\ \dfrac{\text{large parallelogram}}{\text{small parallelogram}} ~~ = \dfrac{6}{2} ~~~ = ~~~~~ \dfrac{c}{3} \end{gather*}
Because the ratio of the short sides is 3 to 1, the ratio of the long sides must also be 3 to 1, so \(c\) must be 9.
\(\blert{\text{Method 2}}\)
Sometimes it is easier to look at the ratios within each figure, like this:
\begin{gather*} \hphantom{long sides} ~~~~~~~~ \text{small} ~~~ \text{large}\\ \dfrac{\text{long sides}}{\text{short sides}} = \dfrac{3}{2} = \dfrac{c}{6} \end{gather*}
With these ratios, we can see that the building factor is \(\alert{3}\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{3}{2} \times \dfrac{\alert{3}}{\alert{3}} = \dfrac{9}{6} \end{equation*}
Again we find that \(c\) must be 9.
