Solution 1.1.12.1.

  1. We write the fraction as
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \dfrac{9~~ \text{out-of-state students}}{32~~ \text{total students}} \end{equation*}
    Out-of-state students make up \(\dfrac{9}{32}\) of Ethan's class.
  2. Let's compute the benchmark fractions with denominator 32.
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{4}~~\text{of}~~32 = 8,~~~~~~~~~~\text{so}~~\dfrac{1}{4}=\dfrac{8}{32} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{2}~~\text{of}~~32 = 16,~~~~~~~~~~\text{so}~~\dfrac{1}{2}=\dfrac{16}{32} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{3}{4}~~\text{of}~~32 = 3 \times 8 = 24,~~~~\text{so}~~\dfrac{3}{4}=\dfrac{24}{32} \end{equation*}
    Now that we have written the benchmark fractions with the appropriate denominator, we can compare our fraction from part (a). We see that the fraction \(\dfrac{9}{32}\) is slightly more than \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) of the class.
