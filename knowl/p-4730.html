In Example 3, we multiplied the numerator and denominator of \(\dfrac{3}{4}\) by \(\alert{2}\) to write the fraction with the denominator we needed, namely 8. When we rewrite a fraction, the number we multiply by is often called the building factor.

\begin{align*} \dfrac{\text{old numerator}}{\text{old denominator}} \amp \times \dfrac{\text{building factor}}{\text{building factor}} \amp\amp = \dfrac{\text{new numerator}}{\text{new denominator}}\\ \dfrac{3}{4} ~~~~~~ \amp \times ~~~~~~ \dfrac{\alert{2}}{\alert{2}} \amp \amp = ~~~~~~ \dfrac{6}{8} \end{align*}
