    Subsection 2.3.1 What is a Variable?

    We live in a world of change. The numbers we use to measure our world change, too. The temperature and humidity of the environment change day-by-day, as do prices on the stock market. We monitor our blood pressure and cholesterol count to detect changes in our health. The connections between variable quantities help us understand the forces that shape our world.

    Example 2.3.1.

    1. Consider the table below. Do you see a pattern that relates Delbert's age to Francine's age?
      Francine's age Delbert's age
      10 16
      13 19
      17 23
      Yes: Delbert's age is always six more than Francine's age.
    2. Write a mathematical sentence that expresses Delbert's age in terms of Francine's age.
      \begin{equation*} \text{Delbert's age} = \blert{\text{Francine's age} + 6} \end{equation*}
    In the example above, Francine's age and Delbert's age are variables.

    Definition.

    A variable is a numerical quantity that can take on different values at different times or in different situations.

    Note 2.3.2.

    Quantities that do not change, such as the six-year age difference between Delbert and Francine, are called constants.

    Checkpoint 2.3.3.

    Did you see the pattern relating the Discount price to the Regular price in Exercise 1? When we look for a pattern we can check several possibilities:
    • Is there a constant number we can add to or subtract from the first variable to get the second variable?
    • Can we multiply or divide the first variable by a constant to get the second variable?
    • Is the sum or the product of the two variables a constant?

    Example 2.3.4.

    1. In the table below, do you see a pattern that relates the total utility bill to your share?
      Utility Bill ($) Your Share ($)
      28 7
      36 9

      \begin{equation*} \text{Your share} = \blert{\text{utility bill} \div 4} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.3.2 Bar Graphs

    We can use a bar graph to display the values of a variable. The height of the bar illustrates the value at each time or in each situation.

    Example 2.3.6.

    The bar graph shows the number of new homes sold each month in Columbus County last year.
    1. What variable is shown in this graph?
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{Number of new homes}} \end{equation*}
    2. How many houses were sold in March?
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{Twenty houses were sold in March.}} \end{equation*}
    3. In which month(s) were 35 houses sold?
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{Thirty-five houses were sold in July and in October.}} \end{equation*}
    4. In which month were the most houses sold, and how many houses were sold that month?
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{The most houses were sold in June; 40 houses were sold that month.}} \end{equation*}
    5. How much did housing sales decline from October to November?
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{Housing sales declined by 25 (from 35 to 10).}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.3.3 Using Letters for Variables

    We often use a single letter to represent a variable quantity. In the next Example, the variables are Jayson's age and Shawna's age.

    Example 2.3.10.

    Shawna was 22 years old when her son Jayson was born.
    1. Fill in the table.
      Jayson's age 1 4 7 12 18
      (Calculation) 1+22 4+22 7+22 12+22 18+22
      Shawna's age 23 26 29 34 40
    2. Explain how to find Shawna's age if you know Jayson's age.
      \begin{equation*} \blert{\text{Add 22 to Jayson's age.}} \end{equation*}
    3. Write a mathematical sentence for Francine's age.
      \begin{equation*} \text{Shawna's age} = \blert{\text{Jayson's age} + 22} \end{equation*}
    4. Let \(\alert{a}\) stand for Jayson's age, and write an expression for Shawna's age in terms of \(\alert{a}\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} \text{Shawna's age} = \blert{a + 22} \end{equation*}
    5. Check that your expression for Shawna's age gives the correct values when we replace Jayson's age by \(\alert{a}\) in the table in part (a).
      \(\blert{a}\) 1 4 7 12 18
      \(\blert{a+22}\) 23 26 29 34 40

    Note 2.3.11.

    A letter used as a variable must always stand for a number. In the previous Example, the variable \(\alert{a}\) stands for Jayson's age, which is a number. We cannot let a variable stand for, say, "Jayson," or for any other person or object.

    A Quick Fractions Refresher.

    • To find \(\dfrac{1}{3}\) of a number, we divide the number by 3:
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{3}~ \text{of}~ 36 = 36 \div 3 = 12 \end{equation*}
    • To find \(\dfrac{2}{3}\) of a number, we first find \(\dfrac{1}{3}\) of the number (divide by 3), then multiply the result by 2:
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{2}{3}~ \text{of}~ 36 = 2 \times 12 = 24 \end{equation*}
      Exercise
      1. Find \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) of 120.
      2. Find \(\dfrac{3}{4}\) of 120.
    • To find 5% of a number, we multiply the number by 0.05:
      \begin{equation*} 5\% ~ \text{of}~ 80 = 0.05 \times 80 = 4 \end{equation*}
    • If a number is increased by 5%, we multiply the number by 1.05:
      \begin{equation*} 80~ \text{increased by}~ 5\% ~\text{is}~ 1.05 \times 80 = 84 \end{equation*}
      Exercise
      1. Find 15% of 60.
      2. Increase 60 by 15%.
